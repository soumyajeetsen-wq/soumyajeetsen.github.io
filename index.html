1. "Irrelevant Leads" is the #1 pain point across all 5 feedback channels — confirmed independently by NPS (34%), App (32%), QRF, BLNI, and 9696 calls

1. Seller Problem Consensus — What sellers are telling us across every channel
IndiaMART collects seller feedback through 5 independent channels at different stages of the seller lifecycle. NPS is asked at 30, 60, and 90 days from onboarding (and every 90 days after) — measuring how sellers feel about the platform at key milestones. App internal feedback is triggered after 5 buy-leads consumed — capturing very early impressions. QRF and BLNI are per-lead quality signals. 9696 captures sellers actively considering leaving. When the same problem surfaces as a top issue across 3+ of these channels, it's a genuine structural problem — not a sampling artefact.

🔬 The Acclimatisation Hypothesis — Why Catalog sellers are unhappy

The data suggests that the IndiaMART platform takes 60-90 days to deliver value — sellers need time to set up their catalog, learn Lead Manager, understand how to respond to leads, and close their first deals. The evidence:

📋 Catalog — monthly, ₹3,000/mo
NPS = 8.1 with 37% detractors. Only 3 sellers recovered from Detractor→Promoter monthly. These sellers are surveyed at 30 days — at which point they've barely learned the platform. Most were likely oversold by the sales agent and are comparing a polished promise to a raw 30-day reality. They churn before the value materialises.
📦 TSCatalog — annual subscription
NPS = 26.2 with the highest D→P recovery (7 monthly, 106 quarterly). These sellers stay long enough to acclimatise. They may be equally unhappy at 30 days, but by 60-90 days they've figured it out. The annual commitment gives them runway to see value — something monthly sellers never get.

The fact that 72 Catalog sellers DID recover in the Oct-Jan quarter proves they can be won back — but likely only those who renewed for a 2nd or 3rd month. The funnel is: oversold → frustrated at 30 days → some churn, some renew → survivors acclimatise and improve.

The core question: Are we selling monthly subscriptions for a product that takes 60-90 days to deliver value? If so, the fix isn't better leads — it's either a) better onboarding to compress the learning curve, b) resetting sales expectations, or c) rethinking the 1-month package entirely.

🔴 The blind spot: Between 5 BLs (too early to judge lead quality) and 30 days (already formed opinions), there's no feedback at the point where sellers have consumed enough leads to genuinely evaluate the platform. A seller at 50 BLs has a real basis for saying "these leads are irrelevant" — unlike one at 5 BLs who may just not have their catalog set up yet. Without this touchpoint, we can't distinguish onboarding friction from real product problems.

💡 What this unlocks: Comparing 50-BL feedback against 30-day NPS for the same cohort answers a critical question. If 50-BL says "leads are relevant" but 30d NPS is still negative → the problem isn't leads, it's something else (billing, expectations, service). If 50-BL also says "leads are irrelevant" → it's a genuine algorithm problem for that seller. This single addition would directly resolve the perception gap identified in Section 3.
Why not 100 BLs? By 100 BLs, you're mostly capturing TSCatalog/Star/Leader sellers — Catalog sellers at ₹3K/month may not reach 100 BLs before churning. The 100-BL signal overlaps heavily with 60/90-day NPS. Recommend adding only after 50-BL proves valuable.
6. What becomes possible with GLID linkage — the next project
Today's dashboard shows aggregate patterns — we can see that Catalog sellers have low NPS and that irrelevant leads are the top complaint, but we can't see if the same sellers who gave QRF "irrelevant" feedback are the ones who became NPS detractors. Linking seller GLIDs across all 6 data sources would unlock a fundamentally deeper level of analysis — and directly answer the open questions from Sections 2-5.
Questions only answerable with linked GLIDs
🔗 Resolve the perception gap
"For sellers who said 'Irrelevant Buyer' in NPS, what was their actual BLNI rate?" — If their BLNI rate is low, the problem is perception/expectations. If high, the algorithm is genuinely failing for these sellers. This is the single most important question to answer.
🔗 Decode the TSCatalog recovery
"What happened to the 106 TSCatalog D→P movers? Did their lead quality improve, did they get more leads, or did AM intervention help?" — Answers whether recovery is organic (platform learning curve) or assisted (human intervention).
🔗 Validate the acclimatisation hypothesis
"Do Catalog sellers who renew for month 2 have better NPS than first-month Catalog sellers?" — If yes, the product works but takes time. If no, even renewers are unhappy, which means the value prop itself is broken.
🔗 QRF as a churn early-warning
"Do sellers who mark leads as irrelevant in QRF subsequently call 9696 or deactivate?" — If yes, QRF becomes a proactive churn prediction tool, not just an algorithm input. Intervene before the seller reaches 9696.
🔗 Catalog seller economics
"How many leads does a Catalog seller consume before becoming a Promoter vs remaining a Detractor?" — Reveals the break-even point. If it's 80 BLs but Catalog sellers only consume 40 before churning, the product isn't broken — the subscription duration is wrong.
🔗 9696 call prevention
"For sellers who called 9696 with 'Not Getting Maturity', what was their actual lead volume?" — Could reveal whether this is a real product gap or an education/hand-holding issue solvable with self-serve tools.
